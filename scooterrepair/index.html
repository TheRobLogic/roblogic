Scooter RevivalA Genuine Buddy Resurrection Guide

From dormant to daily driver

The Patient

A Genuine Buddy scooter — Italian style, Taiwanese engineering, American grit — that's been sitting a while and needs to be brought back to life.

The Genuine Buddy is manufactured by PGO Scooters of Taiwan and sold by Genuine Scooters out of Chicago. Under those retro-mod body panels lives a member of the GY6 engine family — the most common scooter engine in the world. Parts are cheap, knowledge is everywhere, and these machines are built to be revived.

This guide covers everything you need to go from yard ornament to road-ready. Works for the Buddy 50, 125, 150, and 170i — and most of the general steps apply to any dormant scooter.

Identify Your Model

Four engine variants exist across the Buddy line. Knowing which one you have determines your parts, fluids, and procedures.

ModelEngineTypeFuel System
Buddy 5049.7cc Minarelli2-strokeCarburetor
Buddy 125124cc GY64-strokeCarburetor
Buddy 150150cc GY64-strokeCarburetor
Buddy 170i168cc GY64-strokeFuel-injected

All models use automatic CVT transmission — twist and go, no shifting.

How to Tell Check the VIN on the front steering head, under the seat, or on a plate inside the front leg shield. The Buddy 50 has smaller 90/90-10 tires; the 125/150/170 have 100/90-10s. The 50 is a 2-stroke (no oil filter). The 170i has fuel injection — no carburetor. If it's a 4-stroke with a carb, it's a 125 or 150.

Common Issues After Sitting

Fuel System — The #1 Problem

Gasoline degrades after 30–60 days. After months, it becomes varnish that clogs every orifice in the fuel system. The carburetor jets will be blocked. The scooter will not run until they're clean.

  • Varnished fuel clogging jets, filter, lines, and petcock
  • Failed vacuum petcock — rubber diaphragm cracks, causing no fuel flow or constant flooding
  • Deteriorated fuel lines — cracked, hardened, or brittle rubber

Electrical

  • Dead battery — lead-acid batteries left uncharged sulfate and die. If it's been 6+ months without a tender, it's gone.
  • Corroded connections — especially battery terminals, ground wires, and spark plug cap
  • Failed auto-choke — the electric enrichment valve's wax element can fail, preventing cold starts

Mechanical

  • Flat/dry-rotted tires — if sidewalls are cracked, replace them. Don't ride on dry rot.
  • Seized brakes — caliper pistons seize, drum shoes rust to the drum
  • Stale engine & gear oil — absorbs moisture, degrades. Change regardless of mileage.
  • Stuck CVT belt — can take a "set" from sitting in one position. Usually works itself out.

The Revival — Step by Step

Important Work through these in order. Do NOT try to start the engine until Steps 1–6 are complete.
1

Initial Assessment

  1. Center stand, flat surface. Get it stable.
  2. Visual inspection. Cracked plastics, broken wires, rodent damage (chewed wires, nesting material in the airbox), rust.
  3. Check for engine seizure. Try the kickstarter. It should move with compression resistance. Won't budge? Remove the spark plug, squirt penetrating oil into the cylinder. Wait 24–48 hours. Try again.
  4. Check the wiring harness. Look for frayed, cut, or corroded wires. Rodents love scooter wiring.
2

Battery

  1. Remove the old battery (under floorboard or under seat).
  2. Install a new YTX7A-BS (125/150/170i) or YTX7L-BS (Buddy 50). 12V, 6Ah, sealed AGM. Ready to install.
  3. Connect positive (+) first, then negative (-). Clean terminals with a wire brush if corroded.
  4. Test voltage with a multimeter — should read 12.4V–12.8V.
  5. Check the main fuse near the battery. Replace if blown.
3

Fuel System — Drain & Clean

This is the most important step. If the scooter sat with fuel in it, the fuel system is contaminated.
  1. Disconnect the fuel line from the carburetor to stop flow.
  2. Drain all old fuel. Siphon or drain. Dispose properly.
  3. Inspect the tank interior for rust, varnish, or debris.
  4. Replace the inline fuel filter (~$3–5). Always replace, never clean.
  5. Replace fuel lines if cracked or hardened. Use 1/4" (6mm) fuel-rated hose.
  6. Test the vacuum petcock: With the fuel line disconnected and the scooter OFF, fuel should NOT drip. If it does, the diaphragm has failed — replace the petcock (P6523000000, ~$15–25).
  7. Check the vacuum line from intake manifold to petcock. Cracked = no fuel flow.
4

Carburetor Service

For a scooter that's been sitting, a full carb disassembly is mandatory. "Spray some carb cleaner in there" will not cut it.

  1. Remove the carburetor. Loosen the intake manifold clamp, disconnect fuel line, vacuum line, throttle cable, and auto-choke connector.
  2. Remove the float bowl (four Phillips screws).
  3. Remove the float and float needle. Push out the float pin with a small punch.
  4. Remove all jets: main jet (brass, center tower), pilot/slow jet (smaller, to the side), jet needle (inside the slide/diaphragm assembly).
  5. Soak all metal parts in carb cleaner for several hours. Remove rubber O-rings first — carb cleaner destroys rubber.
  6. Blow out every passage with compressed air. Every hole. The tiny pilot jet passage is the most common clog.
  7. Inspect the float needle valve seat. Pitted or dirty = the carb will flood. Replace.
  8. Reassemble with a rebuild kit (~$8–15). Includes new jets, gaskets, float needle, and O-rings.
Alternative A complete replacement carburetor runs $25–45 aftermarket, or $80–120 for an OEM-style adjustable carb from Scooterworks. If the original is severely varnished, a new carb can be faster and more reliable.
5

Spark Plug & Ignition

  1. Remove and inspect the old plug. Black/sooty = rich. White = lean. Wet = flooding. Tan/brown = normal.
  2. Install a new spark plug:
    • Buddy 50: NGK BP7HS — 18mm socket — gap 0.6–0.7mm
    • Buddy 125/150: NGK C7HSA — 16mm socket — gap 0.6–0.7mm
    • Buddy 170i: NGK CR7HSA — gap 0.6–0.7mm
  3. Anti-seize on threads. Hand-tighten, then 1/4 turn with a wrench.
  4. Test for spark: Remove plug, reconnect wire, hold threaded end against engine metal. Kick the kickstarter. Look for a clear blue spark.
No spark? Check in order: spark plug cap connection → ignition coil → CDI unit → stator/pickup coil → kill switch → wiring harness.
6

Oil Change

Engine Oil (4-Stroke Models)

  1. Remove the drain bolt on the bottom of the engine. Drain completely.
  2. Remove and replace the oil filter screen + O-ring.
  3. Replace drain bolt with a new crush washer.
  4. Fill with 0.9L of 15W-40. Check the sight glass — fill to the upper mark.
  5. Run 1 minute, recheck level.

Gear Oil (All Models)

  1. Locate the drain and fill plugs on the rear of the transmission case.
  2. Drain old gear oil.
  3. Refill with 90–110mL of SAE 85W-140. Fill until oil seeps from the fill hole.
Buddy 50 Owners The 50 is a 2-stroke. No oil filter. Fill the separate 2-stroke oil tank with SAE 30 FC-class oil (1.0L capacity). The oil pump mixes it automatically. Running out = engine seizure.
7

Air Filter

  1. Remove the airbox cover (4–5 screws).
  2. Inspect the element. Paper filters (125/150/170i) must be replaced if dirty. Foam filters (Buddy 50) can be washed and re-oiled.
  3. Check for rodent nests, leaves, or debris inside the airbox. Clean thoroughly.
  4. Check the air intake tube running from the airbox under the seat plastics.
8

Tires & Wheels

  1. Inspect sidewalls for dry rot. Cracks = replace. No exceptions.
  2. Check tread depth — minimum 1mm.
  3. Inflate: Buddy 50: F 25 / R 36 psi. Buddy 125/150/170i: F 26 / R 36 psi.
  4. Tire sizes: Buddy 50: 90/90-10. Others: 100/90-10.
  5. Spin each wheel by hand. Check for wobble (bent rim) or grinding (bad bearings).
9

Brakes

Front Disc Brake

  1. Squeeze the lever — should feel firm, not spongy.
  2. Visually inspect pad thickness through the caliper. Minimum: 1mm.
  3. Check the rotor for deep scoring or warping. Light surface rust is normal.
  4. Check brake fluid level in the master cylinder. Top up with DOT 3/4 if low. Dark fluid = flush and replace.
  5. Seized caliper? Remove it, push the piston back with a C-clamp.

Rear Drum Brake

  1. Squeeze the rear lever — should engage with moderate effort.
  2. Adjust the cable using the adjuster nut at the rear hub if needed.
10

Electrical Systems Check

  1. Turn key to ON — dash lights should illuminate.
  2. Check all lights: headlight (high/low), tail, brake (both levers), turn signals, horn.
  3. Replace burned-out bulbs. Clean corroded connectors with contact cleaner.
  4. Check ground wires — especially the main engine ground. A bad ground causes all sorts of gremlins.
  5. Test the starter motor. Clicks but won't turn? Starter may be stuck or battery too weak. Try the kickstarter.
11

First Start Attempt

  1. Fresh fuel. Regular unleaded 87 octane. Add Sea Foam or fuel stabilizer.
  2. Reconnect the fuel line to the carburetor.
  3. Wait 30 seconds for fuel to fill the carb bowl.
  4. Do NOT touch the throttle. The auto-choke handles cold enrichment.
  5. Electric start: 5 seconds max per attempt. 10 seconds between attempts.
  6. No luck? Use the kickstarter. Firm, full kicks.
  7. Fires but dies? Hold about 1/3 throttle while starting — the auto-choke may be shot.
  8. Once running, idle for 2–3 minutes. Initial RPM will be high — that's the auto-choke. Normal.
  9. Watch for leaks under the engine.

Won't Start? Troubleshoot

Fuel

Disconnect fuel line at carb and crank — fuel should pulse out.

Spark

Remove plug, ground against engine, kick — look for blue spark.

Air

Air filter clear? Intake not blocked?

Compression

Kickstarter should have resistance. None = internal issue.

12

Break-In Ride

  1. First ride: 1 mile max, low speed. Test throttle, brakes, steering, lights.
  2. Return and check for leaks (oil, fuel, brake fluid).
  3. Second ride: 3–5 miles. Open it up a bit. Reach full operating temperature.
  4. Re-check tire pressure after tires warm up.
  5. Re-check oil level after the first real ride.

Buddy-Specific Quirks

The Vacuum Petcock

The Buddy has NO manual fuel shutoff valve. A vacuum-operated petcock only allows fuel flow when the engine creates intake vacuum. You can't gravity-feed fuel to test the carb without the engine cranking. To bench-test fuel flow, suck on the vacuum line (or use a hand vacuum pump) while the fuel line is disconnected.

The Auto-Choke

No manual choke lever. Cold starting is handled by an electric autobystarter — a wax-element thermostatic valve that gradually closes over 3–5 minutes as the engine warms up.

If the auto-choke fails Hold your hand over the air intake opening while cranking to restrict air (DIY manual choke). To test: disconnect the auto-choke electrical connector. Starts easier with it disconnected? It's stuck closed. Runs worse? It's stuck open or fine.

GY6 Engine Family = Dirt Cheap Parts

The 125/150/170 engines belong to the GY6 family — the most common scooter engine on Earth. Parts are extremely cheap, widely available, and documented to death on YouTube and forums. The engine is robust and forgiving of neglect.

Idle Adjustment

After carb cleaning, idle will need re-adjustment. Idle speed screw: clockwise = faster, counterclockwise = slower. Target: 1500–1800 RPM. The OEM carb has no accessible air/fuel mixture screw (EPA-compliant). Aftermarket adjustable carbs ($80–120 from Scooterworks) have one and run noticeably better.

Fuel Shutoff Habit

When parking for more than a day, disconnect or pinch off the fuel line. Better yet: after your last ride, let the engine idle until it dies from fuel starvation (~2 minutes with the line disconnected). This empties the carb bowl and prevents varnish buildup.

The Buddy 50 Is Different

The 50 uses a horizontal Minarelli 2-stroke — completely different from the vertical GY6 4-stroke in the 125/150/170. Parts are NOT interchangeable. Different carb (PZ18), different plug (BP7HS), different tires (90/90-10), and significantly slower (30 mph top speed).

Seasonal Storage

  1. Fill the tank completely and add fuel stabilizer.
  2. Run the engine 5 minutes to circulate stabilized fuel.
  3. Disconnect the fuel line and let it idle until it dies (empties the carb bowl).
  4. Connect a battery tender/maintainer.
  5. Inflate tires to max sidewall pressure.
  6. Plug the exhaust opening to prevent moisture entry.
  7. Store indoors or under a quality cover.
