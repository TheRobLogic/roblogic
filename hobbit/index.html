Section 01

History & Background

The Honda PA50 was manufactured at Honda's facility in Aalst, Belgium — Honda's first factory outside of Japan — between 1976 and 1991. In Europe, it was sold as the Honda Camino. When Honda brought it to the US market in 1978, they needed a name with personality.

They picked "Hobbit." Tolkien's novels had saturated American pop culture by the late '70s, and Honda leaned all the way in. Their ad campaign featured an original mascot called "Honda Harold" — not Bilbo, for trademark reasons — described as having "a love of comfort," "a very agreeable nature," and being "a mighty friendly helpful fellow to take on your travels."

The Hobbit arrived during the American moped boom of the 1970s energy crisis. Cheap gas killed the market by the mid-'80s, and Honda pulled the PA50 from the US after 1983. But in Belgium and the Netherlands, the Camino kept rolling until 1991, and a robust tuning culture persists there to this day.

Early color coding: Red = PA50-I (20 mph), Yellow = PA50-II (30 mph). Rob's yellow '78 is the faster variant. The speed difference comes from the carburetor main jet (#60 vs #78), reed valve configuration, and a steeper variator ramp plate angle on the PA50-II.

Why People Love Them

The PA50-II engine has large transfer ports and breathes well compared to other mopeds of the era. With simple modifications — exhaust, carb, variator notching — these engines make real power. A kitted Hobbit with a 70cc cylinder can hit 40–50 mph. Some race builds break 60.

The V-Matic CVT is unique among mopeds. Most used single-speed or two-speed transmissions. The variator gives smooth automatic acceleration and makes the Hobbit a more interesting tuning platform — you can tune the engine and transmission independently.

And then there's the Honda build quality. Even 45+ years later, the frames and mechanicals hold up. Parts fit properly. The fundamentals are sound. There's a reason the Hobbit has a cult following at moped rallies across the country.

How the Variator Works

The CVT uses a set of weighted rollers inside a ramp plate on the front pulley. As engine RPM climbs, centrifugal force pushes the rollers outward, squeezing the front pulley faces together. This increases the effective drive ratio — smooth, automatic acceleration with no gear changes.

The key difference between PA50-I and PA50-II is the variator ramp plate angle. The PA50-II has a steeper slope, letting the rollers push the pulley faces closer together for a higher top speed. Stock rollers are 15×16mm.

Repair & Revival Guide

If your Hobbit has been sitting, here's what to check and fix — in order. Most Hobbits that "don't run" have fuel system problems. Start there.

1. Fuel System (The #1 Problem Area)

Fuel Tank Rust

The single most common problem on any Hobbit that's been sitting. The steel tank rusts internally and sends particles into the petcock, fuel line, and carb.

  1. Drain all old fuel. Inspect inside the tank with a flashlight.
  2. If rusty: fill with white vinegar, let soak 24 hours.
  3. Drain vinegar, rinse with baking soda + water solution to neutralize acid.
  4. Rinse with clean water, then immediately rinse with fresh gasoline to displace water.
  5. Severe cases: use a tank sealant kit (Kreem or POR-15) after cleaning.
  6. Always install an inline fuel filter between petcock and carb, even on a clean tank.

Replacement tanks available at Treatland and Hoop Rider Parts (NOS).

Petcock Cleaning

The petcock has a small internal filter screen that clogs with rust and varnish. This is often the cause of "fuel in the tank but won't start."

  1. Remove petcock from tank (two screws).
  2. Disassemble and clean the internal screen with carb cleaner.
  3. Replace the gasket/O-ring. Check fuel flow with the lever in ON and RES positions.
  4. If it still doesn't flow freely, replace the whole petcock.

Carburetor Rebuild

The stock Keihin PA-type carburetor is described by the community as "the most finicky thing on the bike." Every speck of debris must be removed.

  1. Remove carb from intake manifold (note: intake bolts torque to 80–120 kg-cm / 5.8–8.7 ft-lbs).
  2. Disassemble completely — float, needle, jets, gaskets.
  3. Soak body in carb cleaner for 30 minutes. Blow out every passage with compressed air.
  4. Check the float — if it sticks, the bike will flood or starve. Replace if dented or saturated.
  5. Verify main jet: PA50-II stock is #78. PA50-I stock is #60.
  6. Replace all gaskets. Rebuild kits available at Treatland and 1977 Mopeds.
  7. Reassemble, set float height per service manual, reinstall.

2. Ignition System

No-Spark Diagnostic (In Order)

  1. Spark plug boot — is it tight on the plug? Loose boot = no spark. Push it on firmly.
  2. Spark plug — pull it, ground the threads against the cylinder, pedal and look for spark. No spark? Try a known-good NGK BPR6HS.
  3. Kill switch — make sure it's in the RUN position. Disconnect the kill switch wire and test spark again.
  4. Points (stock ignition) — open the flywheel cover. Points gap should be 0.3–0.4 mm. Clean contact surfaces with fine sandpaper. Replace if pitted.
  5. Condenser — a dying condenser causes arcing at the points. If points look burned/pitted, replace the condenser too.
  6. Ignition coil — test resistance per manual. Replace if out of spec.
CDI Conversion
Eliminates points maintenance entirely. The HPI Mini Rotor CDI kit is the go-to upgrade. Available at Treatland and Moped Division. If you're tired of adjusting points, this is the single best reliability upgrade you can do.

3. Compression Test

Testing

If you have spark and fuel but the engine won't fire, check compression.

  1. Remove spark plug, screw in compression gauge.
  2. Pedal the engine through several revolutions.
  3. Stock compression should be 100–120 PSI. Below 80 PSI = top end rebuild.
  4. Low compression causes: worn piston rings, scored cylinder, blown head gasket, cracked reed valve gasket leaking into crankcase.

4. Top End Rebuild

When and How

If compression is low, the top end needs attention. This is a common job on these bikes.

  1. Remove exhaust, intake, and cylinder head bolts.
  2. Carefully pull the cylinder off the piston. Check the bore for scoring.
  3. Inspect piston rings — replace if worn, stuck, or broken.
  4. Light scoring: hone the cylinder with a ball hone. Deep scoring: new cylinder needed.
  5. Replace gaskets (head gasket, base gasket, exhaust gasket).
  6. Reassemble with fresh gaskets. Torque head bolts per manual spec in a cross pattern.

OEM and aftermarket cylinders, pistons, rings, and gasket kits available at Treatland. For performance: see the Mods section on 70cc kits.

5. Variator & Belt

Belt Inspection & Replacement

  1. Remove the variator cover (left side of engine).
  2. Inspect belt for cracks, glazing, fraying, or uneven wear.
  3. Stock belt: ½" × 42" (OEM part 23100-148-7030).
  4. Performance option: toothed belts reduce slippage. Available at Dos Cycles.

Variator Rollers

Worn rollers affect shift characteristics and can cause flat spots in acceleration.

  1. Remove the variator front pulley (requires a variator holder tool or impact driver).
  2. Inspect the rollers — stock size is 15×16mm. Replace if flat-spotted or worn.
  3. Heavier rollers = shifts to higher ratio sooner (better top speed, slower acceleration).
  4. Lighter rollers = shifts later (faster acceleration, lower top speed).

6. Brakes, Tires, Cables

Brakes

  1. Both front and rear are drum brakes. Check lever freeplay: 10–15mm (⅜"–⅝").
  2. If brakes are grabby or weak, pull the wheel and inspect the shoes. Replace if glazed or worn past the wear indicator.
  3. Clean the drum interior with brake cleaner. Lightly sand the shoes with 120-grit for better bite.

Tires

Stock size is 2.25-17 front and rear. These are a standard moped tire size and still widely available.

  1. Check sidewalls for dry rot (cracking). If the bike has sat for years, replace them even if they hold air.
  2. Inflate to: Front 28 PSI, Rear 20 PSI (per the info plate on this bike).
  3. Tubes are replaceable separately — carry a spare and a patch kit.

Cables

Throttle and brake cables dry out and stick after years of sitting.

  1. Try lubing first — disconnect the cable, drip light oil or cable lube down the housing from the top.
  2. Work the cable back and forth. If it's still sticky or frayed, replace it.
  3. Replacement throttle and brake cables available at Treatland and 1977 Mopeds.

7. Electrical System

Lighting & Charging

The Hobbit's electrical system is simple — a magneto/flywheel generates power, and there's a separate lighting coil.

  1. Headlight, tail light, and turn signals run off the lighting coil. If lights are dim or dead, check the bulbs first.
  2. Check all ground connections — corrosion on ground wires is the most common electrical gremlin.
  3. Horn not working? Usually a corroded wire or dead horn unit.
  4. For wiring diagrams, download the free service manual PDF from Moped Army (98 pages).

8. Crankshaft Seals & Bearings

Advanced Work
This requires splitting the cases. If you're losing compression despite good rings/cylinder, or the engine makes rattling/whining noises, suspect crank bearings or seals. Worn crank seals cause air leaks that lean out the mixture — dangerous on a two-stroke. Stock bearings: 2× 6004 bearings, seals: 2× 15 × 25.5 × 7mm. Test for play by grasping the crankshaft — any wobble means replacement.

Section 05

Modifications

The Hobbit is one of the most tunable mopeds ever made. Here's the upgrade path from stock to screaming, with realistic speed expectations at each stage.

Stock Bone Stock PA50-II

As Honda intended. Reliable, quiet, legal. 25:1 premix, pedal start, wave at your neighbors.

27–30 mph
Stage 1 Bolt-On Basics

Exhaust upgrade — aftermarket pipe (Simonini, Proma, or Leovince) unlocks breathing potential. Re-jet the carb to match (go up 1–2 jet sizes). Notch the variator — the signature Hobbit mod.

Variator Notching

  1. Measure and mark a line 10mm from the outside edge of the variator.
  2. Using a Dremel with a cut-off wheel, cut windows along the roller tracks.
  3. Windows (leaving material at the top) are preferred over full notches — prevents the rollers from getting chewed up by the moving pulley cheek.
  4. This lets the rollers travel further outward, increasing front pulley diameter and top speed.

Variator springs (1000 RPM and 1500 RPM sets) tune when shifting begins, matching your exhaust/engine combo.

35–38 mph
Stage 2 70cc Kit Build

Cylinder kit — DR Racing, Parmakit, Malossi, or Stage6 R/T. All are 46mm bore, and include cylinder, piston, rings, gaskets, and wrist pin. Combined with the Stage 1 exhaust and variator mods, this is where it gets fun.

Also consider: CDI ignition conversion (HPI Mini Rotor), larger carburetor (Dell'Orto or Mikuni), performance reed valve.

40–50 mph
Race Full Race Build

Ported cylinder kit, race crankshaft ("Super Stuffy" cranks available), tuned expansion chamber exhaust, performance variator (TJT or TRICKmetric TRICK6), and a properly jetted big carb. At this point you're building a race moped.

Warning: Notching + big bore puts serious torsional stress on the crankshaft. Use a variator cover. Race cranks are recommended at this level. You'll also want to re-evaluate your brakes, tires, and whether the stock drum brakes can actually stop you from 55 mph.
50–60+ mph
