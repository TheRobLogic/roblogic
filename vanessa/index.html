The 6.2L Detroit Diesel is the same engine block used in the original military Humvee. Slow but unkillable — known for 17–20 MPG and running past 300K miles. The Stanadyne DB2 injection pump is the weak point ($800–$1,500 to replace).

Engine built by Detroit Diesel in Michigan; van assembled at the GM Scarborough plant in Ontario (open 1953–1993). Components crossing the US-Canada border during assembly was standard under the 1965 Auto Pact.

Conversion: Valley Van Works was a small Las Vegas shop doing custom interiors in the 1980s. Carpet walls and floors, insulation, interior trim. No digital footprint survives — they predate the internet and weren't part of the major converter brands (Starcraft, Explorer, Mark III, etc.).

The G-Series: 25 Years, One Platform

The third-generation G-series launched in April 1970 and ran until June 1996 — 25 years on the same basic platform, the longest-produced vehicle platform GM ever designed. The original ad campaign played on the Apollo-era space craze with the tagline "Chevrolet launches the space vehicle", showing the van printed vertically like a rocket.

The 1984 model sits in the 1983–1991 body refresh: rectangular headlamps, redesigned grille, new tilt steering column. The '84 specifically introduced optional swing-out side doors (1/3 – 2/3 split) as a no-cost alternative to the sliding side door.

The G20 is the Chevrolet-badged 3/4 ton van. GMC sold the same vehicle as the G25 (marketed as the "Rally" for passengers, "Vandura" for cargo). Same van, different grille badge and fender trim.

The 6.2L Detroit Diesel: Born from Disaster

This engine exists because of one of the worst engines GM ever made.

In 1978, Oldsmobile introduced a 5.7L diesel V8 for passenger cars. They essentially took a gasoline 350 V8 and converted it to diesel without strengthening the block, heads, or bolts for the higher compression ratios. The head bolts stretched. The heads cracked. The blocks failed. An estimated one in four failed catastrophically. The FTC brokered a massive class-action settlement. The debacle killed consumer interest in diesel cars in America for a generation.

So when GM needed a proper truck diesel, they had Detroit Diesel design one from scratch. Cast-iron block, cast-iron heads, 21.5:1 compression, indirect injection with Inconel prechambers. Purpose-built, not a gasoline conversion. It debuted in 1982.

Wild detail: About 300 prototype 6.2L diesels were installed in 1981 Chevrolet Impala Sport Coupes (two-door cars) and handed to the federal government as long-term testbeds. A diesel Impala coupe — there's something you don't see every day.

Military Service

CUCV Program: The Army adopted the 6.2L for the Commercial Utility Cargo Vehicle program in the early 1980s — militarized Chevy pickups (M1008) and K5 Blazers (M1009). Cold War workhorses at every base in the country. Surplus CUCVs now have a dedicated collector following.

The Humvee: AM General's original HMMWV ran this same engine block, tuned to 185 hp for military use. The engine that powered vehicles through Desert Storm, Afghanistan, and Iraq started life in civilian trucks and vans like this one.

The Good and the Bad

  • Mechanically simple. No electronics on pre-1994 versions. Mechanical fuel injection. You can diagnose most problems with hand tools.
  • Runs on practically anything. The mechanical injection is famously tolerant of biodiesel and waste vegetable oil. Some owners run used fryer oil.
  • 17–25 MPG highway in a full-size van. The gas V8 versions got 10–14.
  • It is slow. 130 hp in a 5,000+ lb van. Highway merging requires planning ahead. Forum consensus: "reliable but slow as molasses."
  • Glow plug problems are the #1 complaint. Essential for cold starts, they fail regularly.
  • Main bearing web cracks on higher-mileage engines. The original cast iron alloy wasn't strong enough for long-term heavy use.
  • The diesel was always a minority option. Most buyers chose the gas 350 or 305 V8. Finding a diesel G-series today is genuinely uncommon — van forums treat them as noteworthy finds.
Pop Culture & Custom Van Scene

The A-Team Connection

The most famous G-series van in history is the 1983 GMC Vandura from The A-Team (NBC, 1983–1987). That black-and-gray van with the red stripe, driven by B.A. Baracus (Mr. T), is this van's identical twin — the GMC Vandura and the Chevrolet G-series are the same vehicle with different badges. The show van was a half-ton G15 with a gas 5.7L V8 and custom spoilers.

When they made the 2010 A-Team movie, they used a 1994 Chevrolet G20 rather than a GMC Vandura, confirming what everyone already knew: same van.

The Custom Van Era

The custom van craze peaked around 1976–1977. TIME Magazine declared vanning "an American craze." The movement had its own magazines (Custom Vans, Truckin', Vans & Trucks), its own events (the National Truck-In drew thousands), and its own aesthetic: airbrushed murals (wizards, sci-fi, pin-ups), chrome side pipes, mag wheels, porthole windows, shag carpet everywhere, velvet upholstery, built-in stereos, and mini-bars.

By 1984, the craze had cooled — the Dodge Caravan minivan (1983) and rising insurance costs were killing the scene. But factory conversion vans were still popular through the '80s. The G20 3/4-ton was the preferred platform for heavier conversion work because the beefier suspension and 12-bolt rear axle could handle a full interior buildout.

The vanning scene never fully died. The Van Nationals still happens annually, and the modern #vanlife movement is the grandchild of the 1970s vanners.

Where She Was Built

The GM Scarborough Van Assembly plant in Scarborough, Ontario has a quirky history:

  • Opened in 1952 building Frigidaire refrigerators. Yes, refrigerators. Converted to auto components in 1963, then van assembly in 1974.
  • Built its one-millionth van in 1986.
  • Employed 2,800 workers at peak production.
  • GM announced closure around Thanksgiving 1989. The last van rolled off the line May 6, 1993.
  • The site is now a shopping mall (Eglinton Town Centre) with a Cineplex movie theater built on the spot where vans used to come together.

What Moves the Needle

  • Rust is everything. A rust-free body is worth 2–3x a rusty one regardless of mechanicals.
  • Injection pump health — the Stanadyne DB2 is the Achilles heel. A recently rebuilt or known-good IP adds real value.
  • The 6.2 diesel doesn't command a premium like a 7.3 Powerstroke or Cummins would. It's slow but gets 17–20 MPG and runs forever.
  • Plain vans are gaining value over beat-up camper conversions — the van-life crowd wants a clean canvas.
  • Low mileage matters. Under 100K is desirable. The 6.2L is typically "tired" around 230K, though some last 400K+.
  • Clear title is essential. Bill-of-sale-only examples sell for pennies.
Realistic private sale for a running, decent example: $5,000 – $10,000. Dealer lots ask $12K–$20K but there's room to negotiate.

Crossing to TJ — Tips
  • Get Mexican auto insurance first. Your US policy doesn't cover you. Baja Bound does online day policies (~$15–25/day).
  • Bring your passport. SENTRI or Global Entry speeds up the return crossing.
  • Bring a Spanish speaker if you can — prices stay lower.
  • Bring reference photos and fabric swatches. Be specific about materials, stitching, patterns.
  • The traditional upholstery district is Calle 2a and 3a, east of Revolución. You can walk between 20+ shops and compare quotes.
  • Cross early (7–8 AM). Return before 3 PM to avoid the worst lines.
  • For a full van interior, plan to leave it overnight. 1–2 days typical. Taller Nuevo FM's surveilled lot is good for this.
  • Option: pull the seats and panels yourself and just bring the parts across. Avoids leaving the whole vehicle.
  • Pay 50% deposit, balance on completion. Inspect thoroughly before paying.
  • If your vehicle is financed or leased, check your loan docs — you may need written permission to take it into Mexico.
