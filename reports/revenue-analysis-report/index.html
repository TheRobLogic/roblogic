Bancroft Bar -- 2025 Ticket Revenue Analysis

Report Date: 2026-03-04
Data Sources: 2025 Gmail inbox (4,461 emails), Google Calendar shows.json (197 events in 2025), band_directory.json
Period Covered: January 1 -- December 31, 2025

Executive Summary

The Bancroft Bar generated $8,376.46 in ticket revenue through the TickPick/Fanimal platform across 39 weekly payout cycles in 2025. This revenue was distributed across approximately 96 ticketed shows out of 114 total music events (84% ticketing rate). The remaining shows were either canceled, walk-up only, privately promoted, or used other platforms.

Revenue was heavily concentrated in a small number of high-draw shows. The top 3 shows accounted for roughly $3,790 (45%) of total annual revenue. The median ticketed show generated approximately $20-30 in TickPick revenue.

Key finding: The Bancroft operates on a 100% door-to-bands model -- all ticket/cover revenue goes directly to performers. The bar retains only the TickPick service fee markup. Bar revenue comes from drink sales driven by show attendance, not ticket revenue. This report therefore tracks throughput that the bar facilitated, not bar income.

What Made the Top Shows Different

Conservative Military Image (3/8, $1,955): Highest-grossing show of the year. $20 presale / $25 day-of pricing -- the highest ticket price at the Bancroft in 2025. The booking thread shows 150 total presale tickets allocated (100 at $20 + 50 at $25). As of March 3, 60 tickets remained, suggesting strong early sales. Booked via text with "Nino," not through the standard booking form.

Angry Samoans (12/20, $1,020): National-level punk act. $20 tickets. The organizer (El Hefe) coordinated a stacked 6-band bill. The Bancroft created 120+ comp/hold tickets through TickPick (4 separate organizer orders for 30-60 blocks), suggesting near-capacity anticipated attendance.

Chasing Wind (3/7, $815): Unusually high for a $10 show -- possibly benefiting from CMI hype the following night. This show was part of the same accumulated $2,770 payment period so the exact split is estimated.

Dr. Know (6/28, $736): Birthday show for James Head. $20 tickets. The booking thread shows the promoter asked for presale money before the show to "pay the bands" -- 18-email thread. Stacked bill with 6+ bands.

Born to Run (4/11, $403): $15 cover. Had the longest booking thread in the dataset (51 emails in the "Re: Tour" thread), suggesting significant coordination effort.

Seasonal Pattern

Revenue is extremely event-driven, not seasonal. Three individual shows (CMI in March, Dr. Know in June, Angry Samoans in December) account for roughly 44% of annual revenue. Removing those three, the remaining ~91 shows generated about $4,665 total, averaging ~$51 per show.

The summer months (July-September) were unexpectedly weak despite a full show calendar. September and November were the worst months. There is no clear seasonal trend -- revenue spikes are driven by nationally known or regionally famous headliners, not time of year.

10. Financial Model & Door Policy

The Bancroft's Standard Terms

From multiple booking emails, the Bancroft's consistent policy is:

"100% of the door (Online & in person ticket sales) go directly to the bands, the only thing the bar takes is the service fee amount that is added to online ticket sales."

  • No guarantees. When asked for a $150 guarantee (by touring act via booking form), the Bancroft responded: "I can't do a guarantee unfortunately, but 100% of the door goes to the bands."
  • Bands set their own cover price. The booker (Kayla/Rob) asks bands: "What you want to set the cover amount at?"
  • The bar's revenue model is drink sales. Show attendance drives bar revenue, not ticket revenue.

TickPick Service Fee Impact

TickPick/Fanimal takes a service fee on online sales (added on top of the ticket price, not deducted from it). The payout amounts to the Bancroft represent the face value of tickets sold. Since 100% goes to bands, the Bancroft's direct revenue from this is $0, but the bar benefits from:
1. Higher attendance from advance ticket commitment
2. Drink sales from attendees
3. Guest list management (85 guest list emails sent in 2025)

11. Other Financial Signals in Email

Guarantee Requests (76 emails mentioning "guarantee")

Most guarantee requests came from touring acts via the booking form. The Bancroft consistently declines, offering 100% door instead. One notable exchange:

  • A touring band requested a $150 guarantee via their booking agent (Mike Yakouba, Last Light Presents NW).
  • The Bancroft responded: "I can't do a the guarantee unfortunately, but 100% of the door goes to the bands."
  • The agent asked: "If so what would the door split be?"

This is the standard dynamic -- touring acts want guarantees, the Bancroft counters with 100% door.

Presale Money Requests

The Dr. Know birthday show promoter (Leila Head) asked: "Would we be able to get the presale money before the show to pay the bands?" This suggests that for externally promoted shows, the promoter collects the door money and distributes it to bands, with the Bancroft acting as venue/ticketing intermediary.

Cover Charge Discussions (85 emails)

Most common discussion points:
- $10 cover: 134 mentions (the default)
- $5 cover: 57 mentions (budget shows)
- $15 cover: 32 mentions (touring acts)

Pay Structure Mentions

  • "100% of the door" appears in 58 outgoing Bancroft emails (it is the standard boilerplate).
  • "Pay the bands" appears in 19 emails, always in the context of door money going to performers.
  • No evidence of any venue cut from ticket/door revenue.

12. Key Takeaways

  1. Revenue concentration is extreme. Three shows generated 44% of all ticket revenue. The typical show generates $20-50 in TickPick presale.

  2. Nationally/regionally known acts are the only reliable revenue drivers. Conservative Military Image, Angry Samoans, and Dr. Know -- all established punk/hardcore acts -- were the only shows to crack $700+.

  3. $20 ticket price is the sweet spot for high-revenue shows. The four $20 shows averaged $930 in revenue vs. $34 average for $10 shows and $19 for $5 shows.

  4. Saturday night is essential. 62% of revenue comes from Saturday shows despite being only ~34% of the calendar.

  5. TickPick presale captures only a fraction of door revenue. Many shows have walk-up attendance that far exceeds presale. The $5-$10 shows with only 1-8 presale tickets likely had 20-50+ walk-up attendees.

  6. Repeat local bands do not build presale audiences. Fungal Mirage, Fancy Faces, Close Enough, and others play frequently but their presale numbers do not grow. Their audiences come walk-up.

  7. External promoters drive the biggest shows. The top 3 shows were all booked through external promoters/organizers, not the standard booking form. Investing in promoter relationships may be more effective than booking more frequent small shows.

  8. November is dead. Only $10 in ticket revenue across the entire month -- the worst performer by a wide margin.
