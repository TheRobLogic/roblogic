A Visual History

Deaf Artists
Through the Decades

1920 — 2020

A century of deaf and hard-of-hearing artists who shaped culture, challenged perceptions, and created enduring works across painting, sculpture, film, poetry, music, and performance.

1920

The 1920s

GR
Landscape Painting

Granville Redmond

1871–1935

Deaf from infancy, Redmond became a leading California Impressionist whose friendship with Charlie Chaplin influenced silent film acting technique. Chaplin gave him a studio on his movie lot, collected his paintings, and cast him in several films including City Lights.

Learn more
DT
Sculpture

Douglas Tilden

1860–1935

Known as the "Michelangelo of the West," Tilden created iconic public sculptures across San Francisco including the Mechanics Monument and California Volunteers. Deaf from scarlet fever at age four, he studied in Paris and became one of America's foremost sculptors.

Learn more
PV
Painting

Petrona Viera

1895–1960

Deaf from meningitis at age two, Viera became the first female professional painter in Uruguay and a central figure in the Planismo movement. The daughter of a Uruguayan president, she proved that a deaf woman could achieve international artistic recognition.

Learn more
1930

The 1930s

JC
Woodcarving

John Louis Clarke

1881–1970

A Blackfeet artist known as "Cutapuis" (The Man Who Talks Not), Clarke was deaf from scarlet fever at age two. Without formal training, he became internationally renowned for his wildlife carvings from his studio near Glacier National Park.

Learn more
CW
Painting & Etching

Cadwallader Washburn

1866–1965

A Gallaudet valedictorian, Washburn created over 1,000 drypoint etchings and served as a war correspondent in three conflicts — remarkable for a deaf person in that era. He studied architecture at MIT and painting under William Merritt Chase.

Learn more
SM
Painting

Shunsuke Matsumoto

1912–1948

A major figure in Japanese modern art who lost his hearing at 13 from meningitis. Known for haunting urban landscapes in cold blue tones, he resisted wartime artistic nationalism while creating deeply personal work. He died of tuberculosis at 36.

Learn more
1940

The 1940s

JC
Drawing & Assemblage

James Castle

1899–1977

Born profoundly deaf in rural Idaho, Castle created art from salvaged paper, soot mixed with saliva, and sharpened sticks. His work has been exhibited at the Venice Biennale, Smithsonian, and Whitney Museum — making him one of America's most celebrated outsider artists.

Learn more
DB
Lithography & Woodcuts

David Ludwig Bloch

1910–2002

Deaf from meningitis at age one, Bloch survived Dachau during Kristallnacht and escaped to Shanghai. His art documented both refugee street life and Holocaust horrors. His archive is held at Gallaudet University — a unique intersection of deaf identity and Holocaust history.

Learn more
RH
Botanical Illustration

Regina Olson Hughes

1895–1993

One of America's foremost botanical illustrators, Hughes created scientific illustrations for the U.S. Department of Agriculture across decades. She lived on Gallaudet's campus for 30 years, deeply embedded in deaf community life.

Learn more
1950

The 1950s

RP
Poetry & Theater

Robert F. Panara

1920–2014

Co-founded both NTID and the National Theatre of the Deaf. A pioneer of deaf culture studies, he developed some of the first plays for deaf audiences. The U.S. Postal Service honored him with a stamp in their Distinguished Americans series in 2017.

Learn more
BB
Acting & Mime

Bernard Bragg

1928–2018

Called "the leading professional deaf actor in the country" by The New York Times. He studied mime with Marcel Marceau in Paris and co-founded the National Theatre of the Deaf in 1967. His NBC special was the first televised deaf performance in sign language.

Learn more
AN
Acting

Audree Norton

1927–2015

A founding member of the National Theatre of the Deaf and the first deaf actress photographed in Time magazine. Her 1974 Kodak commercial won a Clio Award, and her 1978 SAG complaint about casting discrimination sparked nationwide protests.

Learn more
1960

The 1960s

BM
Painting

Betty G. Miller

1934–2012

Known as the "Mother of De'VIA" (Deaf View/Image Art), Miller was the first artist to exhibit work explicitly about the deaf experience. Her landmark Ameslan Prohibited depicted shackled hands with broken fingers. She led the 1989 Gallaudet workshop that created the De'VIA manifesto.

Learn more
GE
Playwriting & Acting

Gilbert Eastman

1934–2006

First deaf person to earn a master's degree in drama. He wrote over 40 plays including "Sign Me Alice," performed at the Kennedy Center, and won an Emmy in 1993 for co-hosting "Deaf Mosaic." A towering figure in deaf theatrical arts.

Learn more
DM
Poetry

Dorothy Miles

1931–1993

Pioneer of British Sign Language poetry and sign language poetry in both the U.S. and U.K. She translated Dylan Thomas for the National Theatre of the Deaf and compiled the first BSL teaching manual. Google honored her with a Doodle in 2024.

Learn more
1970

The 1970s

PF
Acting

Phyllis Frelich

1944–2014

First deaf actor to win a Tony Award, for originating the lead in "Children of a Lesser God" — a play written specifically for her by Mark Medoff. She was elected to the SAG Board in 1991, the first deaf actress so recognized.

Learn more
CB
Painting & Sculpture

Chuck Baird

1947–2012

A co-founder of the De'VIA art movement who spent a decade as set painter and actor for the National Theatre of the Deaf. His paintings incorporating ASL handshapes became iconic, and he established a foundation to support emerging deaf artists.

Learn more
LB
Acting

Linda Bove

1945–

First deaf actress to be a recurring cast member on Sesame Street, portraying Linda from 1971 to 2002 — the longest-running deaf character in television history. She introduced millions of children to sign language and co-founded Deaf West Theatre.

Learn more
1980

The 1980s

MM
Acting

Marlee Matlin

1965–

Won the Academy Award for Best Actress for her debut in "Children of a Lesser God" (1986), becoming the youngest winner in that category and the first deaf Oscar recipient. Her advocacy led to the Television Decoder Circuitry Act of 1990, requiring closed captioning in American TVs.

Learn more
PC
ASL Poetry & Performance

Peter Cook

c. 1963–

Co-founded the Flying Words Project in 1984, pioneering ASL poetry as performance art. He organized the First National ASL Literature Conference in 1992 and has toured internationally for over 30 years, redefining what poetry can be.

Learn more
CV
ASL Poetry & Linguistics

Clayton Valli

1951–2003

The first person to earn a Ph.D. in ASL poetics, legitimizing ASL literature as an academic field. His scholarly work on ASL phonology remains foundational in linguistics, and his nature-imagery poems are classics of ASL literature.

Learn more
1990

The 1990s

CJ
Comedy & Acting

CJ Jones

1950–

A pioneering deaf comedian who became the first Black deaf actor in an international blockbuster (Baby Driver, 2017). He created the Na'vi Sign Language for the Avatar sequels and toured internationally with three one-man shows.

Learn more
DB
Acting

Deanne Bray

1971–

Became the first deaf actor to lead a primetime network drama as the title character in "Sue Thomas: F.B.Eye" (2002–2005). She came up through Deaf West Theatre and the National Theatre of the Deaf, and advocates for early language access for deaf children.

Learn more
SD
Painting

Susan Dupor

1969–

A De'VIA painter known for surreal, emotionally charged imagery depicting the deaf experience. Her works like The Family Dog explore navigating between hearing and deaf worlds. She teaches art at the Wisconsin School for the Deaf.

Learn more
2000

The 2000s

CK
Sound Art & Drawing

Christine Sun Kim

1980–

A groundbreaking artist who reclaims ownership of sound from a deaf perspective. Her work has been shown at MoMA and the Whitney Biennial, and she performed the national anthem in ASL at the 2020 Super Bowl. A two-time TED Fellow.

Learn more
SF
Music

Sean Forbes

1982–

A deaf hip-hop artist who co-founded D-PAN (Deaf Professional Arts Network) to make music accessible. He signed with the label that discovered Eminem, proving that deafness and musical artistry are not mutually exclusive.

Learn more
NR
Painting & Muralism

Nancy Rourke

1957–

The most prominent living painter in the De'VIA movement, known for a bold "Rourkeism" style with symbolic colors tied to Deafhood and liberation. She received the Laurent Clerc Award from Gallaudet University in 2019 for outstanding contributions to society.

Learn more
2010

The 2010s

ND
Modeling & Activism

Nyle DiMarco

1989–

First deaf winner of both America's Next Top Model (2015) and Dancing with the Stars (2016), proving deaf performers can dominate hearing-centric formats. He founded the Nyle DiMarco Foundation for deaf children and directed the documentary "Deaf President Now!"

Learn more
TK
Acting

Troy Kotsur

1968–

First deaf man to win an Academy Award, taking Best Supporting Actor for "CODA" (2022). His decades of work with Deaf West Theatre and the National Theatre of the Deaf built toward a historic win that validated deaf storytelling at the highest level of cinema.

Learn more
CM
Multidisciplinary Art

Chella Man

1998–

A Chinese-Jewish, transgender, deaf artist who became representation that didn't previously exist. They portrayed Jericho on DC's "Titans," created jewelry transforming hearing aids into fashion, and use art to bridge deaf, trans, and AAPI communities.

Learn more
